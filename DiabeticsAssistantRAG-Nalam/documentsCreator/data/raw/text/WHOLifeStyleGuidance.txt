2. Core Principles of a Healthy Lifestyle (WHO Framework)

A healthy lifestyle consists of:

Balanced nutrition

Regular physical activity

Healthy body weight maintenance

Adequate sleep

Stress management

Avoidance of tobacco

Limited alcohol intake

Regular medical screening

3. Healthy Diet Guidelines (WHO Adapted for Indian Context)
3.1 Energy Balance

Maintain energy intake equal to energy expenditure to prevent overweight and obesity.

For Indian adults:

Sedentary male: ~2000–2400 kcal/day

Sedentary female: ~1600–2000 kcal/day

Active individuals: +300–500 kcal

3.2 Macronutrient Distribution (Recommended Range)

Carbohydrates: 45–60% of total calories

Protein: 15–20%

Fat: 20–35%

Fiber: ≥25–40 g/day

For Indians, lower refined carbohydrate intake is strongly recommended due to high insulin resistance prevalence.

3.3 Carbohydrate Guidance (Critical for Indian Population)
Avoid or Limit:

Polished white rice (especially large portions)

Refined wheat (maida)

White bread

Sugary tea/coffee

Sweets (laddu, jalebi, mysore pak)

Sweetened beverages

Fruit juices

Prefer:

Brown rice

Parboiled rice

Millets (ragi, foxtail millet, kambu, jowar)

Whole wheat chapati

Oats

Broken wheat (dalia)

Red rice

Hand-pounded rice

South Indian recommendation:
Replace 50% white rice with millets or unpolished rice.

3.4 Fat Intake

WHO Recommendation:

Total fat <30% of energy

Saturated fat <10%

Trans fat <1%

Limit:

Ghee (large quantities)

Butter

Vanaspati

Deep-fried snacks (bajji, bonda, vada)

Bakery products

Prefer:

Groundnut oil (limited)

Mustard oil

Sesame oil (traditional South Indian use)

Rice bran oil

Nuts (almonds, walnuts)

Seeds (flax, chia)

Avoid reheating oil multiple times.

3.5 Protein Intake

Protein requirement:
0.8–1.2 g/kg body weight (higher if elderly or diabetic)

Indian vegetarian sources:

Toor dal

Moong dal

Channa

Rajma

Black channa

Soybean

Paneer (low-fat)

Curd

Non-vegetarian:

Fish (especially sardines, mackerel)

Egg whites

Skinless chicken

Avoid processed meats

South Indian advice:
Include sambar + vegetable + protein in every major meal.

3.6 Fruits and Vegetables

WHO:
≥400 g per day (5 portions minimum)

Indian adaptation:

2 portions vegetables

1 leafy green

1 seasonal fruit (whole, not juice)

Prefer:

Spinach (keerai)

Drumstick leaves

Bitter gourd

Bottle gourd

Ridge gourd

Cabbage

Beans

Carrot

Tomato

Guava

Papaya

Apple

Limit:

Mango (large portions)

Banana (large portions in diabetics)

Sapota

3.7 Salt Intake

WHO:
<5 grams per day (<1 teaspoon)

Reduce:

Pickles

Papad

Salted snacks

Processed foods

High salt increases:

Hypertension

Stroke risk

Kidney disease

3.8 Sugar Intake

WHO:
Free sugars <10% of total energy
Preferably <5%

Avoid:

Sugary chai multiple times daily

Sweet coffee

Sweets after every meal

Sweet beverages

4. Physical Activity (WHO 2020 Guidelines)

Adults (18–64 years):

Minimum:

150–300 minutes moderate activity/week
OR

75–150 minutes vigorous activity

Plus:

Muscle strengthening 2 days/week

Examples (Indian context):

Brisk walking

Cycling

Traditional farming work

Yoga

Surya Namaskar

Climbing stairs

Household work

South Indian practical advice:
30–45 min brisk walking daily + weekend strength training.

Sedentary risk:
Sitting >8 hours/day increases diabetes risk.

5. Healthy Weight and BMI

For Asian Indians:

Normal BMI:
18.5–22.9 kg/m²

Overweight:
23–24.9

Obese:
≥25

Waist circumference risk:
Men >90 cm
Women >80 cm

Central obesity is very common in South Indians.

6. Diabetes Prevention and Control

High risk factors in Indians:

Family history

Sedentary job

High white rice consumption

Abdominal fat

Poor sleep

Stress

Targets (general diabetic adults):

HbA1c:
<7% (individualized)

Fasting glucose:
80–130 mg/dL

Post-meal glucose:
<180 mg/dL

Annual:

Eye exam

Kidney function test

Lipid profile

Foot exam

7. Sleep Guidelines

Adults:
7–9 hours per night

Poor sleep increases:

Insulin resistance

Weight gain

Blood pressure

Avoid:

Late-night heavy dinners

Screen exposure before bed

Caffeine after 6 PM

8. Stress and Mental Health

Chronic stress increases:

Cortisol

Blood sugar

Blood pressure

Healthy coping:

Meditation

Yoga

Prayer

Community engagement

Talking to family

Physical activity

Avoid:

Emotional eating

Smoking

Alcohol misuse

9. Tobacco and Alcohol

WHO:
No safe level of tobacco.

Avoid:

Smoking

Chewing tobacco

Gutka

Pan masala

Alcohol:
If consumed, limit strictly.
Better: Avoid completely for diabetics.

10. South Indian Meal Structuring Guidance

Healthy Plate Model (Adapted):

Plate division:

50% vegetables

25% whole grains/millets

25% protein

Example South Indian Meal:
Breakfast:
Idli (2 small) + sambar + vegetable

Lunch:
Brown rice (1 cup) + sambar + poriyal + curd

Dinner:
Millet dosa + chutney (minimal coconut) + vegetable

Avoid:
Large white rice portions + no vegetables.

11. Special Considerations for Indian Population

Indians:

Develop diabetes at lower BMI

Have higher visceral fat

Higher insulin resistance

Therefore:

Earlier screening (age >30 if risk factors)

Lifestyle intervention is primary treatment

Weight loss of 5–7% reduces diabetes risk significantly

12. Key Behavioral Recommendations

Eat at fixed times

Avoid overeating at night

Walk after meals (10–15 minutes)

Reduce portion size gradually

Replace refined carbs with whole grains

Track weight monthly

Monitor blood sugar if at risk

13. Clinical Red Flags (When to Refer Doctor)

Persistent fasting glucose >130 mg/dL

Frequent urination + thirst

Sudden weight loss

Chest pain

Non-healing wounds

Severe fatigue

14. Public Health Priority for India

India has one of the highest numbers of diabetes cases globally.
Urban South Indian populations show higher prevalence due to:

Sedentary IT jobs

High polished rice intake

Low protein intake

High refined carb consumption

Lifestyle modification is the primary prevention strategy.
